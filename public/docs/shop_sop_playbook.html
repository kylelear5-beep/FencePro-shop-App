Running Tasks & Receiving

Goal: All material is received for accounting and stored within 24 hours of delivery.

💡 Why This Matters

Receiving is the front door of every job. If material isn’t checked in accurately, accounting can’t reconcile costs, the office can’t schedule installations, and crews show up to a job site missing what they need. Every hour material sits un-received increases the risk of lost, damaged, or misattributed inventory. Catching errors at the truck — before it drives away — is the cheapest fix we’ll ever get.

Running Tasks: Receiving hours are 9am to 12pm. Receive any material that comes in. Inspect when it arrives. If obviously damaged OR the wrong material, call supplier directly and don’t take off from truck. Notice broken/low material, hardware, accessories and notify GM. Note issues in BOM.

Receiving Checklist

  1. Find PO: Find purchase order for the delivery. If none, contact buyer (Current SFR buyer: Chris Wiliams).
    • Reason: The PO is our single source of truth for what we actually ordered. Without it, you have no way to verify if the delivery is correct and accounting has no paper trail.
  2. Match PO: Match purchase order to delivery slip. If issue, contact vendor and/or SFR buyer.
    • Reason: Vendors make mistakes — wrong quantities, substituted items, partial shipments. Catching discrepancies now prevents a crew from arriving at a job site missing critical material, which delays the install and costs the company money.
  3. Truck Inspection: On flat bed, in box truck, or “delivered good”.
    • Good: All material matches delivery slip and is not broken. Put in its location.
    • Wrong material: Contact vendor asap.
    • Broken material: Take photos, write down damage on BOL, contact vendor asap (cc buyer). Make decision on credit/replacement based on job need.
    • Reason: Documenting damage before the driver leaves is critical. Once the truck pulls away, the vendor can claim it left their warehouse in perfect condition. Photos and written notes on the BOL are your legal proof for credits or replacements.
  4. Unload & Load: Unload, perform further inspection if needed. For stock material, put in appropriate bay. For job lot material, put in free location or bay for staging.
    • Reason: Storing material in the correct spot immediately means staging is faster, inventory counts are accurate, and nobody wastes 30 minutes searching the yard for something that got dropped in the wrong bay.
  5. Sign Off:
    • Received: Initial PO with Date, initials, ‘all good’. Staple delivery slip/BOL to back, put in ‘received folder’.
    • Received error: Describe error on PO, put in Error folder.
    • Received STI: Needs further inspection to verify it’s okay.
    • Reason: The signed-off PO is the official record that closes the purchasing loop. If the paperwork isn’t done, accounting can’t pay the vendor, can’t reconcile job costs, and we lose visibility into what we actually have on hand.
✅ Expected Outcome

Every delivery is verified against its PO, inspected for damage, stored in the correct location, and signed off within 24 hours. Accounting has clean paperwork, the yard inventory is accurate, and no crew is ever surprised by missing or broken material on a job site.

Material Check Task

Goal: Make sure the appropriate material is in the yard so a job can be scheduled.

💡 Why This Matters

The Material Check is the gate between “sold” and “scheduled.” The office cannot schedule an installation until Fence360 shows the material is confirmed on-site. If you mark it complete without physically verifying, we risk scheduling a crew, sending them to a job, and discovering on-site that key pieces are missing — wasting an entire day of labor and damaging the customer’s trust. A thorough material check also catches purchasing errors early, giving the buyer time to reorder before the job date.

Purpose: Material checks are a Fence360 task used when a product needs to be ordered to verify the material is present. The material must be physically checked and completed in Fence360 to queue the office to schedule the installation.

  1. Get the BOM:
    • Get the BOM(s) from the files. (Sometimes there are multiple BOMs).
    • Note that the BOM needs to be verified… NOT the purchase order.
    • Reason: The BOM reflects what the job actually needs. The PO reflects what was ordered, which may be a different quantity or specification. You are checking reality against the job requirement, not against someone else’s order.
  2. Check Yard & Accessories:
    • Check the exact material quantities in the yard and accessories.
    • If anything is missing, create a custom task and assign to SFR buyer (Chris Williams) with the issue.
    • If everything is present, mark completed in Fence360.
    • Reason: Physically walking the yard and counting is the only way to confirm material is truly here. Systems can show inventory that’s already been staged for another job, damaged, or moved. Your eyes are the final confirmation. Flagging shortages immediately gives the buyer maximum lead time to source replacements.
✅ Expected Outcome

Every job that moves to “scheduled” has physically confirmed material in the yard. Zero crews arrive at a job site missing material. Shortages are flagged to the buyer days or weeks before the install date, giving maximum time to resolve.

Staging

Goal: All material, except concrete, including gates and accessories is staged in a bay and double counted by 2pm the day before.

💡 Why This Matters

Staging is where planning meets execution. A properly staged job means the crew walks in at 5:30 AM, loads in 20 minutes, and arrives at the customer’s home with every single piece they need. A poorly staged job means the crew calls back at 8 AM asking for missing posts, the customer waits, and we eat the cost of a second trip. The color-coding system and double-check process exist because a single missed aluminum insert or wrong latch can halt an entire installation. Every minute spent staging carefully saves 10 minutes of chaos on the job site.

Success Musts: Counted material sheet in ‘confirmed kill’ folder. All hardware in the tote with job name marked in blue tape. Job packet in tote. If trailer present on Friday, Monday’s job is loaded and strapped by Friday at 2pm.

Next Level Success: Material is staged 1 week in advance. Concrete is staged.

  1. Print & Color Code BOM:
    • Orange: tote items (small hardware, fasteners, consumables).
    • Blue: concrete (staged separately, often loaded last).
    • Pink: gates, aluminum inserts, loose items (easy to forget, high-impact if missing).
    • Green: important job notes or specific tools on the job package diagram.
    • Reason: Color coding transforms a dense BOM into a visual checklist. It prevents you from accidentally skipping categories of material and lets the second-checker verify at a glance that every color group has been addressed. It also helps when staging multiple jobs — you can quickly see which category you’re pulling for.
  2. Verify & Collect:
    • Verify BOM for accuracy (aluminum inserts for gate posts, hardware counts, caps, latches, etc.). Report issues to GM on chat.
    • Put large material in bay. Pull all hardware, caps, consumables into tote with customer last name.
    • Put job packet in the job tote. Put count on the BOM with initials and date next to items, put in ‘ready for 2nd check’ folder.
    • Reason: Writing your initials and counts on the BOM creates personal accountability and a paper trail. If something is wrong on the job site, we can trace it back to exactly where the process broke down and fix it — not to blame, but to improve.
  3. Double Check:
    • Second person completes double check.
    • If all material is present, put in confirmed kill file. If error, correct error AND note on the BOM.
    • Reason: No human catches 100% of their own mistakes. The double-check is an intentional safety net. The “confirmed kill” folder means that job has been verified by two sets of eyes and is guaranteed ready. This is the standard that lets the GM sleep at night.
✅ Expected Outcome

Every job staged to 100% by 2pm the day before. Two people have verified material counts. The foreman finds a perfectly organized bay, a labeled tote with all hardware, and a job packet ready to go — no phone calls, no delays, no surprises.

✓ DO

  • Wrap loose pickets in plastic.
  • Band all like materials together.
  • Ensure material is on pallets or stickers.

✗ DO NOT

  • Bend pickets when strapping (use corner braces/edge protectors).
  • Send dinged or damaged material out.

Loading

Goal: Load material securely and safely. Foreman doesn’t need to add added strapping or deal with double ends. Material is easy to unload at the site.

💡 Why This Matters

A badly loaded trailer is a safety hazard, a liability, and a time killer. Improper weight distribution causes trailer sway and fish-tailing at highway speeds — that’s a potential accident. Loose material that shifts during hard braking can damage product (meaning a re-order and a delayed job) or fall off the trailer entirely. If the foreman has to re-strap or rearrange material at the job site, that’s 30–45 minutes of paid labor wasted in front of the customer. Loading it right the first time protects people, protects product, and protects the schedule.

🚛 Loading Rules & Safety

  1. Safety Checks: Trailer brake check, Tire pressure check.
    • Reason: Brakes and tires are non-negotiable. A brake failure with 3,000+ lbs of material behind you is catastrophic. This check takes 2 minutes and prevents the worst-case scenario.
  2. Weight Distribution:
    • 80-90% of weight over the axle(s). Heavy material and material used last on axle (concrete + pickets typically).
    • 10% - 20% on the tongue. No significant weight behind the axle.
    • Tongue down is okay. Never tongue up.
    • Reason: Physics. Too much weight behind the axle lifts the tongue, reducing steering control on the tow vehicle and causing dangerous sway. Tongue-heavy is stable; tongue-light is a fishtail waiting to happen. Putting heavy/last-used items over the axle also means the crew unloads lighter items first without unstacking the whole trailer.
  3. Placement & Strapping:
    • Parallel deck from wheels to front of trailer.
    • Loaded so it can be removed easily at the job (e.g. never put pickets on top of posts because posts are used first).
    • Two straps per pallet. Extra strap wrapped tightly on ratchet side.
    • All ratchets on same side. Ensure material will not become free if hard braking.
    • Reason: “Load in reverse order of use” means the crew grabs what they need first without moving everything else. Two straps per pallet is the minimum to prevent shifting — one strap can work loose over a bumpy road. Ratchets on the same side means faster unstrapping at the site.
✅ Expected Outcome

Every trailer leaves the yard road-safe and load-balanced. The foreman arrives at the job site, unstraps, and starts unloading immediately in the correct order. Zero material damage in transit, zero re-strapping on site, zero safety incidents on the road.

Shop and Yard Org

Goal: Fast staging, easy inventory count, easy to identify low supply. Job isn't done until it's cleaned up.

💡 Why This Matters

A disorganized yard is a silent cost center. When material doesn't have a home, you waste time searching for it during staging. When bays are blocked, forklift operators take longer routes or double-handle pallets. When low inventory isn't noticed, a job gets delayed because we ran out of caps or brackets. An organized yard isn't about looking nice — it directly controls how fast you can stage, how accurate your inventory counts are, and how quickly you can spot problems before they become emergencies.

  • Mark trailer locations for easy loading/unloading. Each material has defined home(s).
    Reason: Defined locations mean anyone can find anything without asking. It also means returned material goes back to the right spot automatically.
  • See something, do something. Utilize your steps (bringing trash when walking across yard).
    Reason: Small messes compound into big problems. Picking up as you go prevents the "big cleanup" that eats an hour at end of day.
  • Direct forklift access to heavily used items.
    Reason: If you have to move 3 pallets to get to vinyl rails, that's 15 minutes of wasted motion per staging job. Multiply by 5 jobs a week and you've lost over an hour.
  • Notify GM if any material is low; when in doubt, notify.
    Reason: Material lead times can be 1–3 weeks. Catching low stock early gives the buyer time to reorder before it becomes an emergency that delays a job.
  • Key Items: Magnet sweep once per week. Cut vinyl returns go in the racks. Full wood and vinyl goes back in their bay.
    Reason: Loose screws and nails puncture tires (costly downtime). Vinyl returns in racks preserves usable material and prevents damage. Organized returns = free inventory.
✅ Expected Outcome

The yard is clean and organized every day. Any team member can find any material in under 2 minutes. Inventory counts are fast and accurate. Low stock is flagged before it becomes a crisis. The shop looks professional — the kind of operation customers and vendors respect.

Crew Morning

Get out by 7:20 on days where job is starting

💡 Why This Matters

The morning departure sets the tone for the entire day. A crew that leaves late arrives late, starts late, and may not finish the job — which means a second trip, overtime, or an unhappy customer. The 7:20 target exists because most jobs are scheduled to start at 8:00 AM. Every role has a specific checklist so that nothing is left to memory at 5:30 AM — checklists don't forget things, tired people do.

  • Foreman: Trailer safety (Weight distribution, Strapping, Connections, Lights, Tires). Get unique job details from Operations Manager. Tool check, Consumables.
    Reason: The Foreman is legally responsible for the trailer on the road. They must personally verify safety. Getting unique job details now (access issues, slope, dog in yard) prevents surprises on site.
  • Co-Foreman: Materials check: material, consumables, fasteners. Customer call with ETA.
    Reason: The Co-Foreman's material check is the final line of defense before leaving the yard. The customer ETA call sets expectations and confirms someone is home to open the gate.
  • Installers: Work with co-foreman to double check material. Work with Production to get concrete if necessary. Get unique tools.
    Reason: Three sets of eyes on material means near-zero chance of leaving something behind. Getting concrete now means Production isn't interrupted later for an emergency delivery.
✅ Expected Outcome

Every crew departs by 7:20 AM with verified material, a safety-checked trailer, and the customer already called. The foreman knows the job details, the team has the right tools, and there are zero "we forgot X" phone calls back to the shop.
